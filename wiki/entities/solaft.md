Solaft (formerly Albany Filtration Technologies) is an industrial filtration business that became a defining psychological and financial episode in [[Bill Maiden]]'s life. Originally an ANZ Private Equity portfolio company, it was acquired by Bill and his former boss Justin in a leveraged buyout following ANZ PE's wind-down. The business and its associated legal and tax events are a primary source of Bill's shame, hypervigilance, and fear of exposure.

## ANZ PE Origins

Albany Filtration Technologies was one of 52 portfolio companies managed under ANZ PE. During ANZ's wind-down of its PE division from 2009, Bill worked under Eddie Law to sell off the portfolio. Solaft began "wobbling" — the CEO and CFO were removed, and Bill became deeply involved operationally.

## The Tax Wound (2011)

Bill led an equity restructuring of Solaft: converting a large redeemable preference note (RPN) to equity. On paper, the documents allowed it. But Bill later realized — on his own, before anyone else — that this conversion would likely trigger commercial debt forgiveness, making the wiped debt taxable "income" for the company. He feared the resulting tax bill could be fatal to the business.

He sat with this fear and shame alone for a period before eventually raising it with his boss Justin. The matter surfaced through audit/tax returns. External directors relied on available tax losses and took the position it was manageable.

Psychologically, this locked in as:

> *"I nearly blew up a company and hundreds of jobs because I didn't foresee the tax. If people really looked under the hood, I'd be exposed as the guy who nearly destroyed everything."*

Bill carried enormous guilt, fear of humiliation, and hypervigilance from this point forward. See [[Achievement-Based Self-Worth]].

## The Buyout

ANZ decided to sell Solaft. Rather than selling to a third party, Bill and Justin bought it:
- ANZ had originally paid ~US$50m
- Bill and Justin put in ~US$200k equity
- ANZ lent ~US$12m to fund the buyout

A significant driver of this decision was the tax wound — owning Solaft felt like containing the mess and the risk of future blame. Bill felt he had no real choice; he had to "wear it."

He left ANZ PE at this point, taking his redundancy and incentive payout.

## Harvard and Resentment

During the Solaft period, Bill completed the Harvard Business School General Management Program. The experience gave him a taste of possibility and prestige. Returning to Solaft afterward felt like "being dragged back into a cage" — he didn't want to be the guy running an industrial filtration business born from a mistake and fear.

## Insolvent Trading Fear (2014–2015)

By 2014–2015, Bill believed Solaft might be trading insolvent:
- Large customers' timing issues
- China market downturn
- Cash crunch

He repeatedly pushed concerns at board level. He felt unheard and terrified of going to jail for insolvent trading. In June 2015, he resigned as a director — legally prudent, emotionally brutal. He felt he was abandoning people and failing them.

The company later recovered enough not to collapse. This left Bill with a confusing mixture of "maybe I over-reacted" and "no one listened to how dangerous this was."

## Legal Proceedings (Post-Exit)

According to the wealth plan document, years after Bill's involvement ended, he was dragged into proceedings because of historical documents — proceedings relating to conduct that occurred *after* his involvement ended. He took separate representation when his interests diverged from his former co-investor's. The matter resolved through insurers with no admission of liability. The emotional residue — shame, betrayal, the sense of being the one who carries the cost — remains significant.

## Financial Outcome

Bill exited Solaft for ~$6m total. Justin later sold the business for ~$80m — a comparison Bill uses as evidence of his narrative that "things do better after I'm gone." Solaft litigation also cost Bill ~$100k.

## Psychological Legacy

Solaft is central to understanding Bill's decision-making:
- Hypervigilance about tax, legal exposure, and insolvency
- Fear of being "exposed" or humiliated if scrutinized
- Tendency to take on burdens alone before raising them
- Evaluation of every new opportunity through a lens of "could this trap me again?"
