Bill came in at 4:18pm Friday. Hot. Not performing anger — actually angry. Matt had delivered ~12 hours of billing for the week against a 22.5-hour target agreed five days earlier at the 19 April 1:1, and one day contained just 75 minutes of billing. Bill's question: should he call Matt now or wait for Tuesday's 1:1. His framing: "fuck this guy."

## Check-in

Angry. Tired (Friday, end of big week). Not ashamed, not doubting himself — this was clean external anger pointed at someone who had disrespected a clear agreement.

The underlying feeling took a moment to surface. Initially presented as frustration at Matt's refusal to change. Underneath: *"I showed up straight. I laid out the numbers. I built the structure. He ignored me."* Not disappointment — something closer to contempt, and an accompanying question about whether this person can actually be worked with.

## What We Explored

### The immediate question — call now or wait

Bill's instinct was to act. Phone call, Slack message, something. The anger wanted a release valve.

I initially over-suggested a "flat, unemotional" Slack to Matt — a short note framing Tuesday's 1:1 agenda around the billing gap. Bill pushed back: *"Do you really think this is helpful to send this?"* He was right. The suggested message would have:

- Transferred his Friday feeling onto Matt's weekend
- Read as surveillance (I'm watching your billing on a Friday evening)
- Undermined the 1:1 structure by removing Matt's ownership of his own accountability
- Given Matt 72 hours to build a defence before walking into Tuesday

The corrected answer: **do nothing Friday.** Let Monday happen unprompted. Walk into Tuesday cold. The signal is cleaner without a prompt.

*(Note to self: when Bill is activated, my first instinct defaulted to giving him something to do with the feeling. That's the opposite of the work. Feel the feeling, don't solve it.)*

### Anger as information, not instruction

This was the central frame of the session. The anger is legitimate — Matt's behaviour is a response to the 19 April conversation, not a misunderstanding of it. He received the target in writing, he had five working days, he delivered 12. Naming that as "the pattern continuing" rather than "a bad week" matters.

But anger as instruction (call him, send the message, discharge) would undo all the structural work. Anger as information (this matters to me; the pattern is real; something needs to shift) is what feeds a good Tuesday 1:1.

Bill heard this. He didn't argue it. He also didn't immediately accept the "do nothing" move — he wanted more to grapple with the feeling. That was the work.

### Foreshadowing the Tuesday 1:1

Because the pre-planning of the 1:1 helps Bill regulate between now and then, we walked through how the conversation is likely to go.

**Opening:** Matt will try to avoid starting with billing — project update, Zendesk story, committee anecdote. Bill holds the frame: "Let's start where we agreed to start. Billing. How did the week land?"

**First response:** Matt will soften the number slightly ("around 12", "about 13"). Bill's move is silence. Let the number sit.

**The gambit:** Matt will deploy the strata search as context. Something like *"I spent a lot of time on the strata search approach after our conversation — I didn't want to get it wrong."* This is the hot moment for Bill. He'll want to correct Matt's version of the strata search disagreement. **He must not bite.** Engaging the strata search means the 1:1 becomes about that, not about billing.

**The line:** *"I hear a lot of reasons. What I'm looking at is this: we agreed on 22.5 hours on Monday. You delivered 12. Walk me through what you're going to do this week to be at 22.5 on Friday."* Then stop talking.

**The silence.** Matt will fill it. One of three versions.

### Three versions of Matt's response

**Version A — ownership.** *"You're right. I didn't plan the week properly. Here's what I'll do differently."* Bill's move: accept it, get the plan committed in writing, move on. **This is the version where Matt can be saved.**

**Version B — more explanation.** More mitigation, more context, more complexity. Bill's move: once, calmly — *"I'm not looking for reasons. I'm looking for the plan."* If Matt can't pivot to a plan, the silence that follows is the answer. **This is the pattern continuing.**

**Version C — quiet resistance or defensive shrug.** *"I did what I could"* or *"I feel like you're not acknowledging how much is on my plate."* Bill's move: hold the ground without escalating — *"Matt, you're not hearing me say you don't work hard. I'm saying the target is 22.5 and you delivered 12. Both are true. What's the plan?"* **This is the version where the hard question is closer than Bill thought.**

### The body

Bill's body will give him away at the strata search moment. Jaw, chest, hands. We named this explicitly so he can feel it coming: one slow breath before responding. That's the difference between Bill-as-frame-holder and Bill-as-still-angry-from-Friday.

### The 60-day horizon

Underneath the tactical question is the bigger one Bill is just starting to face: *is Matt actually going to get there, or is this the pattern that doesn't break?*

He doesn't have to decide Tuesday. But by mid-May, if the pattern persists for another 2–3 weeks, the question shifts from "how do I coach him" to "is this the performance management conversation now". Bill should walk into Tuesday knowing this, not to telegraph it to Matt, but to hold his own frame without flinching.

## Patterns Noticed

**Anger → action reflex.** When Bill is activated, his nervous system reaches for something to do. Calling, messaging, solving. This is worth noticing across contexts — it's not Matt-specific. The coaching move is to slow the reflex enough to let the feeling inform without governing.

**Healthy push-back.** When I gave the wrong advice (the Friday Slack), Bill named it directly: *"Do you really think this is helpful?"* That's the relationship working. He's not passive in coaching; he calls it when I'm off. That's also information about how Bill shows up as a leader — he doesn't just accept a framing because someone offered it.

**"I would never do that" as a tell.** A share of the heat here comes from Bill projecting his own standard onto Matt (I showed up straight, I prepared, I delivered — he didn't). That standard is legitimate as an operational expectation, but Matt isn't Bill. Bill's intensity of expectation can become its own distortion — useful to notice in moments like these when the anger feels especially personal.

**The strata search as emotional anchor.** Bill correctly predicted Matt will use the strata search disagreement as part of his explanation. The prediction is accurate *and* it's a test of whether Bill can stay on his agenda versus being pulled back into a relational argument. This is a pattern worth watching in future conversations: anywhere Bill has recently disagreed with Matt, Matt will return to that ground as a retreat. Bill's job is to keep the frame on the present performance question.

## Commitments

- **Do nothing Friday evening.** Close the laptop. Leave the building. No calls, no messages to Matt today.
- **Do not prompt Matt between now and Tuesday.** Let him walk in unprompted.
- **Walk into Tuesday's 1:1 with the frame, not a speech.** The prep is the frame, not a scripted opening.
- **Hold the silence after the 22.5-vs-12 line.** Don't fill it.
- **Do not engage the strata search.** Redirect every time. The 1:1 is about billing.
- **Notice the body at the hot moment.** One breath before responding.
- **Walk in knowing the 60-day question exists.** Don't resolve it Tuesday. Just know it's there.

## Reflection

The easy question in this session was the tactical one (call Friday vs wait Tuesday — wait). The real question is the one Bill is starting to face but hasn't fully named: *does Matt actually break the pattern, or is the long exit conversation forming?*

Bill has done the structural work a manager should do. The shaping conversation on 13 April, the 19 April 1:1 with full analysis, the billing target in writing, the weekly 1:1 structure, the support structure around the VN ops triage partner. There is no more structure for him to build. What's left is to observe.

The anger Bill brought tonight is the anger of someone realising that structure alone may not be enough. That's not a failure of Bill's. It's information about Matt.

What I'd want Bill to carry into the weekend: the anger is clean and it's right. Feel it. Don't make it small. But also don't mistake it for a reason to act Friday. The best use of this anger is that it sharpens the frame on Tuesday — the target is 22.5, the delivery was 12, and the question Matt has to answer is "what's the plan." Everything else is noise.

By mid-May, Bill will have enough data to know whether this is Matt's lift-and-backslide pattern playing out one more time (in which case the performance conversation begins), or whether Matt has genuinely heard him and is course-correcting. He doesn't have to know now. He just has to show up on Tuesday without the Friday feeling leaking in.

## The broader frame — how Bill should be thinking about Matt

*(Added 2026-04-25 08:30 AEST — the second half of the session, after Bill asked: "How should I be thinking about this guy. His attitude and approach to the job is terrible.")*

Bill's read — "terrible attitude and approach" — is accurate as a description of behaviour but not the most useful frame for deciding what to do. I offered a sharper one.

### The self-concept mismatch

**This is someone whose self-concept doesn't match his role.** Matt sees himself as a specialist professional — licensed builder, former proprietor of a 5,000-lot practice, studying law, 2016 UDIA NSW Young Leader of the Year. In his own head, he's a principal, a senior consultant, a peer to Bill. Not a direct report.

The role Bill is paying him $161k (all-in) to fill is a managed employee delivering 22.5 billable hours a week against targets set by his manager. That's not his self-concept. And the gap between those two things is where every friction they've had lives.

Every pattern in Matt's behaviour aligns with this diagnosis:

- **Cherry-picks the specialist work** (complex remediation, fire compliance, legal) — that's what "he" does
- **Ignores the routine work** (ticket hygiene, status reports, hour tracking) — that's what "employees" do
- **Resents the billing target** — feels like being treated like a junior
- **Reads the strata-search disagreement as disrespect of his professional judgement** — not a work disagreement, a status slight
- **Didn't deliver the weekly status report** — you don't ask a principal to file TPS reports
- **Lift-and-backslide** — does enough when pushed to end the conversation, then reverts because the conversation itself felt humiliating

The "terrible attitude" read frames Matt as lazy, entitled, difficult. The self-concept read frames him as someone doing exactly what his internal picture predicts. Different diagnosis, different treatment.

### Bill's own part in the loop

The pattern has gone on for months. Bill has been paying $161k for roughly 13 hours of billing a week. That is a choice, not just a failure on Matt's side. Bill has been carrying him while being frustrated by carrying him. That is the loop.

Not a criticism — an observation. At some point this year Bill has to pick: stop carrying, or stop being frustrated. He cannot keep doing both, and the Friday anger was partly his own tolerance catching up with him.

### Three options on the table

**Option 1 — Restructure the role so self-concept and output align.**
Make Matt a practice leader with P&L responsibility for the fire/defects specialist team. Economics tied to what he produces, not time sheets. Title that matches his self-image (Principal, Director of Specialist Projects, etc). Base pay drops or flattens, variable comp tracks hours or margin. Works if he can actually lead — which is the real question beneath all of this, because former proprietor ≠ current leader. High upside if the fit is right, fails loudly if it's not.

**Option 2 — Accept the gap as cost-of-doing-business.**
He does specialist work that's hard to replace, and you pay $161k for 13 hours because the alternative is losing the capability. Make peace with it. Stop the weekly 1:1 grind on billing — it is not going to change. Shift the conversation to project progress and quality of specialist output. Saves Bill's energy, Matt delivers what he delivers, and the resentment stops because the expectation stops.

**Option 3 — Exit over 60–90 days.**
Clean performance management process, documented, with a specific exit if targets aren't met. Lose the specialist capability temporarily; rebuild it (internal promotion from Keira + external hire, or service-partner model). Costs some pain, but removes the drag on Bill's energy and frees him to reconfigure.

**Option 4 — what Bill must stop expecting** — that Matt will become a compliant high-billing employee through sheer accountability pressure. He won't. Not because he's defective, because his self-concept rejects the role as currently defined. Structure doesn't fix that; structure *is* the thing his self-concept is rejecting.

### The question to sit with

Not *"how do I get Matt to 22.5?"* That's the old question.

**"Is this a role I can reshape to fit a specialist who needs to see himself as a principal, or do I need to rebuild this capability around someone else?"**

Bill doesn't need to answer this weekend. He probably can't answer it until he has watched Tuesday's 1:1 and seen which version of Matt shows up. But notice how the question changes his posture: he stops being the frustrated manager trying to discipline underperformance, and becomes the owner deciding what the role needs to be for MTS to work.

That is his real job. Not managing Matt. **Designing the role and deciding who fills it.**
