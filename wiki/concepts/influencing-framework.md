The Sterling Insights Influencing Framework is a structured model for planning and executing influence conversations, developed by Sterling Black with James McLaren and Jessica Apfel. It provides a 6-step process for preparing and delivering influential communication, supported by a communication pyramid structure.

> **Document timeline:** This document is undated and is general leadership training material, not Bill-specific content. It represents professional development material acquired as part of broader leadership skill-building. It is not tied to a specific business context but is applicable to the stakeholder management, investor communication, and advisory conversations that appear throughout the MTS and brand strategy work.

## The 6-Step Influencing Model

| Step | Name | Description |
|------|------|-------------|
| **1** | Plan | Define your objective, know your audience, prepare your position |
| **2** | Questions | Open with questions to understand the other party's position before stating yours |
| **3** | Paraphrase | Reflect back what you heard to confirm understanding and build rapport |
| **4** | Present | State your position clearly, using the communication pyramid structure |
| **5** | Overcome objections | Acknowledge, explore, and address resistance |
| **6** | Commit | Secure a specific, actionable commitment — not just agreement in principle |

## The Communication Pyramid

The framework uses a four-level pyramid for structuring any influential communication (presentation, recommendation, proposal):

```
         Hook
         ↓
    Conclusion
         ↓
Three Key Points
         ↓
  Restate Hook & Conclusion
```

**Hook**: Open with a provocative question, surprising statistic, or compelling scenario that creates immediate relevance for the audience.

**Conclusion**: State your conclusion or recommendation *upfront* — don't build to it. Tell them what you want them to believe or do before you explain why.

**Three Key Points**: The evidence, reasoning, or arguments that support the conclusion. Limit to three to maintain clarity and memorability.

**Restate Hook & Conclusion**: Close by returning to the opening hook and restating the conclusion — reinforcing the core message.

> The pyramid is *bottom-up in logic* (conclusion rests on three points) but *top-down in delivery* (lead with conclusion, then support). This is the inversion that makes executive communication effective.

## Preparation Template

The framework includes a structured preparation template for influence conversations:

- **My objective**: What specific outcome do I want from this conversation?
- **Their likely position**: What do I know or expect about where they stand?
- **Opening questions**: What will I ask before presenting my position?
- **My position (pyramid)**: Hook → Conclusion → Three Points → Restate
- **Anticipated objections**: What resistance should I prepare for?
- **Commitment I'm seeking**: What specific action or decision am I asking for?
