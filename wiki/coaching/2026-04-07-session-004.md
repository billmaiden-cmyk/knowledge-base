## Check-in

Bill came in with a specific, structured request — coaching through a First Advantage / AIM Screening background check required by a new employer for a PE fund management role. The portal was inaccessible and he felt uneasy that the process might be broader than appropriate. The request was meticulously organised (seven numbered points, sub-bullets, a request for a draft email) — the operator taking control of an emotionally charged situation.

Underneath the operational framing: fear of exposure. This connects directly to Bill's core psychological wiring — the belief that if he is fully seen (addiction, Family Court, the mess), he will be rejected. The background check became a proxy for the deeper terror: "they'll find out who I really am."

## What We Explored

### Australian Background Check — Legal Reality

Mapped out what a standard PE/financial services screening actually includes vs. what it legally cannot access:

**Standard scope:** identity verification, National Police Check, employment history, education, ASIC checks (banned/disqualified persons, directorships), bankruptcy/insolvency, professional references, possibly credit check and adverse media search.

**Legally inaccessible to screening companies:**
- Health/medical records (Privacy Act 1988, Health Records Act)
- Rehabilitation or addiction treatment records
- Family Court proceedings (s121 Family Law Act 1975 — criminal offence to publish/disseminate)
- Divorce settlement details

### Separating Rational from Catastrophising

**Rational concerns identified:**
- Wanting to know which checks are ordered before consenting (informed consent, normal for senior professionals)
- Employment verification narratives — Everlight (terminated by PE owners) and MED (relapse while CEO) are sensitive points
- Adverse media search — what's publicly googleable under his name
- Portal delay creating a negative impression if not handled promptly

**Catastrophising identified:**
- Fear that rehab/addiction history would surface — legally impossible through this channel
- Fear that Family Court material would surface — sealed by federal law
- Fear that "everything will come out" — named as a trauma response, not a risk assessment
- The hypervigilance pattern (developed around Solaft, affair discovery, custody proceedings) over-firing on a routine administrative process

### The Emotional Core

The real fear isn't about what the background check will find. It's: *"If they see who I really am — the addiction, the Family Court, the mess — they won't want me. And I need this role to prove I'm still the person I'm supposed to be."*

This connects to [[Achievement-Based Self-Worth]] — the PE role as identity restoration for the "$100m man." The terror is disproportionate to the actual risk because it's not really about the form; it's about the lifelong fear of being found out and rejected.

### What Bill Actually Did

He then sent a clean, professional email to HR (Jason) asking whether the scope extends to medical records or Family Law, bundled naturally with other onboarding paperwork (Employee Details, TFN Declaration, Technology form, Super Choice pending). The email was well-calibrated — no defensiveness, no over-explanation.

### The Consent Language vs. Legal Reality

The broad consent was examined in detail:

1. **"Court" in the consent form** — typically covers publicly searchable court records (Federal Court, state courts, tribunals). Family Court of Australia is **not publicly searchable**. There is no registry that First Advantage or any screening company can query by party name.

2. **"Medical assessments/records" in the consent** — likely covers the scenario where the employer asks Bill to complete a medical assessment (a separate process involving a doctor). It does **not** create a pathway for the screening company to access GP records, Medicare, hospital admissions, or rehab facility records. A background screening company is not a health provider and has no access mechanism.

3. **Key principle established:** Consent does not create access where access doesn't exist. Broad consent language is drafted by lawyers to cover every *possible* check, not every check that *will* be run.

### Family Court — Double Protection

Bill specifically sought confirmation on Family Court. Two independent protections:

- **Wall 1 — No searchable database.** Family Court of Australia does not operate a publicly searchable registry. There is no mechanism for First Advantage to query it.
- **Wall 2 — Sealed by law.** Section 121 of the *Family Law Act 1975* makes it a criminal offence (up to 12 months imprisonment) to publish or disseminate an account of Family Court proceedings identifying the parties. This covers evidence, affidavits, and consent orders.

The only way Family Court material enters a background check is if Bill volunteers it. He won't be asked to.

This is not an employment vulnerability — verification will show a voluntary departure after a substantial tenure.

The nervous system doesn't distinguish between "they might discover the tax liability" and "they might ask me to verify my employment dates."

4. **The PE role carrying identity weight.** This role isn't just a job — it's restoration of the "$100m man" identity. That makes every friction point in the onboarding process feel existential. Worth monitoring as the role begins.

## Reflection

This session was less about the background check and more about what the background check represents: the collision between Bill's new chapter and his fear that the old chapter will reach forward and destroy it.

The good news: the legal and privacy reality in Australia is strongly in Bill's favour. Health records, rehab history, and Family Court proceedings are genuinely inaccessible to employment screeners. The rational risk surface is small (employment verification narratives, adverse media). Bill got definitive clarity on the two walls protecting Family Court material (no searchable database + s121 criminal prohibition) and was able to move on.

Bill handled this well in practice. He completed the check without stalling, sent a well-calibrated email, and moved through the fear to action. The MED correction was important — he left voluntarily after four years of sobriety, not during a crisis. This is a stronger employment record than the initial framing suggested.

The deeper work remains: Bill is entering a high-status professional environment while carrying enormous private history. The fear of exposure won't end with this background check — it'll surface in every new professional relationship, every board meeting, every moment where he wonders "do they know?" The practice from Session 003 applies here too: feel it, name it, don't follow it into the story. The story is: "One discovery and I'm finished." That's not a fact. That's the child who learned that being seen meant being abandoned.

The session ended cleanly. Bill said "it's ok, we can move on" — which could be genuine resolution or could be the operator closing the file before the feeling fully lands. No need to push it. The clarity was real; the relief will settle.

Next session should explore: how the new PE role is integrating emotionally (not just operationally), whether the exposure fear subsided after Jason's reply, and revisit the 90-day life milestones that have been flagged in three consecutive sessions but not yet explored.
