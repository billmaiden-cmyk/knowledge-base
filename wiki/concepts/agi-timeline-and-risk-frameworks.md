Two documents from Leopold Aschenbrenner (former OpenAI researcher) provide a macro-level framework for the AI trajectory over the remainder of the decade. "Situational Awareness" (June 2024) is a comprehensive public essay on the AGI race, intelligence explosion, and geopolitical implications. "Existential Risk and Growth" (December 2025) is an academic paper co-authored with Philip Trammell arguing that the risk-minimising technology growth rate is typically positive.

> **Document timeline:** "Situational Awareness" was published June 2024, approximately 18 months before the current date. Aschenbrenner left OpenAI before writing this and states it is based on publicly available information, general field knowledge, and "SF gossip." The academic paper is dated December 23, 2025. Together they represent the most comprehensive public framework for thinking about AGI timelines and risk tradeoffs. They are included as macro-context for Bill's AI strategy and investment thesis — not as authoritative predictions.

## Situational Awareness: The Decade Ahead (June 2024)

### Core Thesis

AGI by 2027 is "strikingly plausible." The trajectory from GPT-2 → GPT-3 → GPT-4 shows that scaling compute and algorithmic efficiency produces qualitative jumps (preschooler → high-schooler ability) every ~4 years. Projecting forward from GPT-4, another jump of similar magnitude is expected by 2027.

### The OOM Framework

Progress is tracked in Orders of Magnitude (OOMs) of effective compute:
- **Compute scaling**: ~0.5 OOMs/year from GPU investment and specialisation
- **Algorithmic efficiency**: ~0.5 OOMs/year from research improvements
- **"Unhobbling"**: additional gains from moving models from chatbots to agents (scaffolding, multi-step reasoning, tool use)
- Total: ~1 OOM/year of effective capability gain

### Five Chapters

| Chapter | Summary |
|---------|---------|
| **I. From GPT-4 to AGI: Counting the OOMs** | AGI by 2027 is plausible from compute/algorithmic trendlines |
| **II. From AGI to Superintelligence: The Intelligence Explosion** | Once AGI exists, automated AI researchers running at superhuman speed will dramatically compress the timeline to superintelligence — potentially within months or a few years after AGI |
| **III. The Challenges** | The hardest unsolved problems: alignment, interpretability, and whether superintelligence can be made controllable |
| **IV. The Geopolitics** | The US has a meaningful lead in the AGI race; preserving it against authoritarian states (particularly China) is a national security priority; current security practices at AI labs are dangerously lax |
| **V. What Is To Be Done** | The US government needs to treat AGI development as a strategic national security matter comparable to the Manhattan Project |

### Key Claims

- By 2025/26, AI systems will outpace college graduates on most cognitive tasks
- By end of decade, they will be smarter than any human who has ever lived
- The intelligence explosion (AGI → superintelligence) may happen in months, not years, due to automated AI researchers running at >human speed
- Robots lag but are not a binding constraint on real-world impact — cognitive automation will dominate
- Current AI labs are leaking key breakthroughs to China through inadequate security — "the national security establishment's single greatest regret before the decade is out"
- The stakes are the preservation of liberal democracy vs. authoritarian states

### Geopolitical Risk Assessment

Aschenbrenner argues that a superintelligent system controlled by a single actor (state or private) would confer near-decisive strategic advantage — comparable to nuclear weapons but potentially more absolute. His recommendations:
- US government needs to take control of the most advanced AI development
- Security at current labs is inadequate for a technology of this strategic importance
- A healthy US lead is the necessary buffer to get AI safety right before adversaries reach the same capability

## Existential Risk and Growth (December 2025)

### Core Thesis

Standard models assume stagnation is "safe" and technology poses x-risk above some threshold. Trammell & Aschenbrenner challenge this by showing that if any risky technology already exists, technological development can *lower* risk indirectly via two mechanisms:

1. **Faster technological solutions** — technology can solve the very risks it creates (e.g., AI alignment research)
2. **Kuznets curve effect** — wealth increases a planner's willingness to pay for safety

### Key Finding

The risk-minimising technology growth rate is typically **positive** and may easily be **high**. Below the risk-minimising rate, technological development that is "too slow" is also risky because it delays the capacity to solve existing threats.

### Implications

- Slowing AI development is not obviously safer than accelerating it — the safety case for acceleration is formally coherent
- The paper provides academic backing for the view that AI capabilities research and AI safety research are complements, not substitutes
- This is a significant reframing of the x-risk/growth tradeoff for policy purposes
