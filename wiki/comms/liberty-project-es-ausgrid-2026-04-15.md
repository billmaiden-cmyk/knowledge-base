Outbound email from Bill Maiden to the Ausgrid divisional CEO, sent 15 April 2026. Purpose: equip the divisional CEO to make the case to the Group CEO for engaging Liberty as management-side advisor to the Ausgrid smart meters spin-out. Context: Group CEO has previously said "not yet" to Liberty engagement.

## Email — Full Text

> Hi,
>
> As foreshadowed in my previous email, I've put together some information, including a targeted overview deck (attached) that you can use with your CEO and other stakeholders.
>
> I think it would also be a good time to introduce us at [investment bank]. Your CEO will commonly look to them for advice.
>
> Below are some talking points explaining why your CEO (and, by extension, the Board) would engage us and why now is the right time.
>
> **When a selling Board engages us to represent their management team, we deliver by:**
>
> **Turning management's reinvestment into an asset for the process**
> Shortlisted PE or PE-backed bidders often require management's commitment and reinvestment alongside them—both as a prerequisite for their participation and as a clear signal of support for their bid. When bidders have a documented financial commitment from "insiders", their confidence increases through to final bid and beyond.
> At the same time, the investment and partnership agreement with bidders is multi-faceted and complex. Management is understandably hesitant to make a substantial financial commitment without a full understanding of the terms. In an optimised process, management will have agreed key terms with all bidders submitting a final offer. Achieving this during a competitive sale requires a structured process and independent corporate advice.
> Liberty enables management to efficiently assess and agree on those terms and thereby give the bidders confidence through to final bid.
>
> **Adding scarce bandwidth to the CEO**
> The CEO's role in a sale process is unique and all-consuming. Key elements—such as keeping the business performing and presenting the plan to bidders—can only be done by the CEO.
> At the same time, the agreement struck between the buyer and the CEO, on behalf of the broader management team, is likely to be one of the most financially significant events in many executives' careers. It also shapes the relationship with the new owner and sets the cultural tone for retaining, motivating, and attracting talent.
> High-quality CEOs will necessarily devote scarce bandwidth to negotiating these unfamiliar agreements with sophisticated counterparties. Market terms are opaque and vary significantly, making these negotiations challenging even for experienced executives. Liberty provides specialist support and a structured process, materially reducing the time and effort required from the CEO. This allows them to focus on the issues only they can resolve.
>
> **Reducing completion risk**
> Without a structured, parallel process, buyers often require a preferred-bidder period or exclusivity to negotiate the management agreement. Where management is critical to the business, this can extend the timeline or introduce "conditions precedent" relating to reinvestment terms.
> Time is the ultimate deal-killer. The longer a process runs, the greater the risk that unforeseen circumstances jeopardise completion. In addition, when management and buyer are left to negotiate directly, misaligned expectations, transaction fatigue, anxiety, and lack of experience or intermediation can prolong or even derail discussions.
> Liberty's process mitigates these risks through structure, discipline, and experienced support.
>
> **Delivering on the Board's obligations to its management stakeholders**
> Ensuring management are well supported through unfamiliar and high-stakes negotiations is an important responsibility of a well-functioning Board.
> Management must negotiate an agreement that may affect their careers for many years. Meanwhile, their counterparties are highly experienced and well advised.
> Liberty enables the Board to ensure management receive appropriate support to safely navigate the process and reach completion. This is both fair and reasonable, and a prudent step by the Board.
>
> **When is the right time for the management team to engage Liberty:**
>
> Transactions are inherently destabilising for teams. Bringing them along and providing support and education delivers better outcomes.
>
> - Engaging early removes risk (rather than adds to it). A better-informed, energised management team will sell the business more effectively and deliver a stronger outcome.
> - Processes move fast, and buy-in can't be retrofitted. The team needs to be prepared so that every engagement with bidders and their advisors lands well. They are being assessed from the very first interaction.
> - When the team understands clearly what the other side looks like for them personally, which could be life-changing, it materially shapes how they turn up and deliver in the process. Engaging Liberty early helps the team understand the opportunity in the deal, not just the work-load. Their enthusiasm rubs off, and their commitment is valued.
> - Without guidance, teams sometimes default to fear. The unfamiliarity and uncertainty of the process causes self-protection and guarded responses. Buyers price that in negatively vs. conviction and confidence. Engaging Liberty early engenders confidence and a feeling of being well supported in early interactions.
>
> The warm-up and education should start now, before bids land and the real work and scrutiny begins. There is no downside if the process is delayed or doesn't conclude.
>
> I hope these points are helpful. I'm happy to talk you through them or set up a meeting with anyone relevant.
>
> Kind regards

## Strategic Assessment — v1 (Original)

*(See [Liberty — Management-Side Advisory Key Messages & Objection Handling](../concepts/liberty-management-advisory-messaging.md) for positioning framework)*

The email covers all four Liberty delivery pillars (management reinvestment as asset, CEO bandwidth, completion risk, Board obligations) and the early-engagement timing argument. Strong content; written as a capabilities pitch to a decision-maker rather than as a tool for an internal champion to advocate upward.

**Key issues identified in review:**
- Reads as a pitch TO the divisional CEO, not a brief FOR him to use upward
- Four talking points is too many for verbal relay — they blur together
- "Not yet" rebuttal buried at the bottom when it's the stated objection
- Language too polished to paraphrase ("multi-faceted and complex," "engenders confidence")
- Investment bank introduction positioned as casual aside rather than strategic move
- Process description implied sequential negotiation (incorrect — see below)

## Email — v3 (Refined Draft, 15 April 2026)

*(Three iterations: v1 original → v2 restructured as brief → v3 process corrections + conviction chain sharpened)*

> **Subject: Talking Points for [Group CEO]**
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> As promised, I've attached the overview deck and set out below how I'd position this with [Group CEO].
>
> I understand his instinct to wait. The reframe is straightforward: **we're removing risk, not adding it.**
>
> **Management conviction is the most valuable signal in the process**
>
> When management invests alongside a bidder, it tells them: *"I believe."* Bidders know that management will always know more about this business than they can learn in six weeks of diligence. That conviction signal — backed by a documented financial commitment from insiders — gives bidders confidence through to final bid and beyond, and it directly strengthens deal outcomes.
>
> But management can't give that signal authentically if they don't understand what's expected of them, whether their capital is at risk, or what the terms of their investment actually are. And in a transaction of this scale, the incentive pool can be life-changing — but if no one has explained it, management can't conceive of it, let alone get behind it. Without that understanding, you get caution. Bidders read caution as risk.
>
> **What Liberty does**
>
> We help management understand how these processes work, what the incentive structures look like, and how the counterparty thinks about it — because we were the counterparty. We've sat in the PE sponsor's chair with our own equity at stake. That lets us explain what's genuinely valuable, what's negotiable, and where the real leverage sits.
>
> From that, we develop a complete package of terms — incentive program, investment structure, good leaver provisions, employment terms — designed to keep management and the financial sponsor well aligned across the deal lifecycle. A management term sheet is loaded to the data room alongside the SPA for bidders to respond to as part of their final bids. That doesn't mean every term is agreed — but it sets the playing field and delivers a far more advanced, structured and disciplined approach to getting the final negotiation done with the preferred bidder.
>
> **Adding bandwidth to the CEO**
>
> The CEO's role in a sale process is all-consuming. Keeping the business performing, presenting the plan to bidders — these can only be done by the CEO. At the same time, the management package negotiation is likely one of the most financially significant events across the leadership team's careers, with sophisticated counterparties where market terms are opaque and vary significantly. Liberty takes that workstream off the CEO's plate with specialist support — freeing them to focus on the things only they can do.
>
> **Why now, not later**
>
> - **Management is being assessed from the first interaction.** A team that understands their own opportunity presents with conviction. A team in the dark defaults to self-protection. Bidders price the difference.
> - **Conviction can't be retrofitted mid-process.** The warm-up and education needs to happen before bids land and real scrutiny begins. There is no downside if the process is delayed or doesn't conclude — but there is clear downside to starting late.
>
> **[Investment bank]**
>
> I'd suggest we introduce ourselves to [bank]. [Group CEO] will naturally look to them, and it's better they have context on what we do and why it supports the process than form a view without it. Happy to coordinate.
>
> The attached deck has more depth if he wants it. This is also a straightforward way for the Board to demonstrate it's supporting management through an unfamiliar, high-stakes process — which is both prudent and fair. Happy to talk any of this through or join a meeting if useful.
>
> Kind regards

## Email — v4 (Final Working Draft, 17 April 2026)

*(Key corrections from v3: "commitment" replaces "confidence/conviction" as lead concept; CEO bandwidth + Board obligation folded into single supporting paragraph; chain lands explicitly on price; headed sections kept but three not four; $150M number absent throughout)*

> Hi [Name],
>
> Thank you for staying engaged on this. I want to give you what you need for the conversation with [Group CEO], including the targeted overview deck attached that you can use with him directly.
>
> I understand the current read is "not yet." I'd ask you to put one reframe to him: the foundation that makes this process work well — for the Board, for management, and for the outcome — takes time to build. The right moment is before bids land.
>
> **Management reinvestment signals conviction — and conviction drives price**
>
> PE buyers operate with limited information. They have weeks of diligence; management has years. Buyers know this asymmetry exists, and they look to management's financial reinvestment as the clearest signal available to them: do the people who actually know this business believe in it enough to back it with their own capital?
>
> That signal directly affects price. When bidders have documented commitment from management, they compete harder and stay engaged through to final bid. When they don't — or when management seems uncertain — they price the doubt in.
>
> The problem is that management are being asked to make a significant financial commitment in a process they've never navigated, with terms they don't understand, against counterparties who do this for a living. Without support, they can't commit with the clarity and conviction that makes the signal credible. Liberty resolves that.
>
> **Management don't know what's possible — and when they do, everything changes**
>
> This is a separate issue, and in many ways the more important one.
>
> Most management teams have never had the economics of a PE transaction explained to them. They don't know what a well-structured incentive program can deliver — the quantum, the mechanics, how good leaver terms work, how their investment is protected. They arrive at these processes focused on the risk and the disruption, not the opportunity.
>
> When Liberty works with a management team — walking them through what is genuinely possible, advocating for the best possible package, developing terms that deliver outcomes for them while driving alignment with the financial sponsor — their orientation shifts completely. From something being done to them, to something they want to make happen. That shift is genuine, and it shows in every room they walk into.
>
> **Protecting completion**
>
> Without a structured parallel process, management terms typically get deferred to a preferred-bidder period. That introduces timeline risk and conditions precedent at exactly the wrong moment. Liberty runs management terms in parallel across all shortlisted bidders so that by the time final bids are submitted, documented commitment is already in place. Time is the ultimate deal-killer. This removes that risk.
>
> Two further points: the CEO should not be spending their best energy on this negotiation — their focus needs to be on running the business and presenting the plan to bidders. And the Board has a genuine obligation to ensure management are properly supported through the most consequential negotiation of their careers, against highly experienced counterparties. Liberty is how you discharge both.
>
> **On timing**
>
> Engaging early removes risk — it doesn't add to it. There is no downside if the process is delayed or doesn't conclude. The only risk runs in one direction.
>
> I'm happy to talk you through any of this, or to set up a meeting with [Group CEO] or other relevant stakeholders whenever the timing suits.
>
> Kind regards,
>
> Bill
