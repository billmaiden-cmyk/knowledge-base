Liberty's management-side advisory practice positions the firm as the trusted advisor representing the management team's interests during PE-led transactions. This page captures key messages, objection responses, and positioning language for use across deals. It is a living sales asset — updated as new objections and deal contexts emerge.

## Core Positioning

Liberty advises the management team — not the seller, not the buyer. The engagement is structured so that management has independent, experienced counsel whose only obligation is to them.

The foundational differentiator: Liberty's principals have been the CEO and the management team in PE transactions. They have sat in the room not as agents advising from the sideline, but as principals with their own equity, their own management packages, and their own careers at stake. They know what matters to the PE sponsor when structuring management terms — what is genuinely valuable, what is negotiable, and what is noise — because they have lived it from both sides of the table.

## How Liberty's Process Works

*(Updated 2026-04-15 ~18:30 AEST, Project ES email refinement session)*

Liberty's workstreams run in parallel with the sell-side process. Some are **consequential** (education → conviction → investment signal → deal outcomes) while others are **discrete** (term sheet drafting, data room preparation, investment structuring). The process is NOT sequential — Liberty does not negotiate terms with each bidder before final bids. The correct mechanics:

### What Liberty does alongside the process

1. **Educates management on how the process works** — what to expect, what the timeline looks like, who the counterparties are, what the key decision points will be.
2. **Gives insight into the value of incentives** — helps management understand the terms they will likely need to agree to, and where negotiation leverage exists. Explains how the counterparty (PE sponsor) thinks about these terms — because Liberty's principals *were* the counterparty.
3. **Develops a complete package of terms** — MIP (management incentive program), investment structure, good leaver provisions, employment terms — designed to keep management and the financial sponsor well aligned across the deal lifecycle, through ups and downs. The goal is enduring alignment, not just closing-day economics.

### The data room mechanism

A **Management Term Sheet** (with square brackets around economics) is loaded to the data room alongside the SPA. This is for bidders to respond to as part of their final bids. Loading the term sheet to the data room **does not mean terms are agreed** — it sets the playing field and delivers a far more advanced, structured, and disciplined approach to getting the final negotiation done with the preferred bidder.

**Outcome:** Management knows what their terms will be from whichever bidder the seller selects. There will be a subsequent negotiation to finalise terms with the preferred bidder, but it starts from a much more advanced position — not from scratch under time pressure.

*(Source: Bill Maiden, 15 April 2026 — correcting earlier description that implied pre-negotiation with each bidder)*

## The Commitment Chain — Management Investment as Deal Signal

*(Updated 2026-04-15 ~18:30 AEST, Project ES email refinement session; terminology corrected 2026-04-17, Project ES v4 session)*

**Terminology note:** The correct lead concept is **commitment**, not confidence or conviction. Confidence is a downstream effect. What bidders require, what they read, and what affects price is management's financial commitment — the documented act of putting capital alongside the buyer as a signal of insider belief. Always lead with "commitment" in positioning language. *(Corrected 2026-04-17)*

This is the central value argument for Liberty's engagement and should be the lead message when positioning with selling boards.

### The chain

1. **Management's investment signals "I believe."** When management commits capital alongside a bidder, it is the most powerful signal in the room. Bidders know that management will *always* know more about the business than any buyer can learn in six weeks of diligence. A documented financial commitment from insiders gives bidders confidence through to final bid and beyond — and that confidence directly strengthens deal outcomes.

2. **But management can't give that signal authentically without understanding.** Management doesn't know what is expected of them. They don't know if their investment is at risk, what the terms are, or what the upside looks like. Without that understanding, they can't commit with conviction — and forced or uninformed commitment is worse than none.

3. **Liberty unlocks the understanding that makes conviction authentic.** By educating management on the process, the terms, the counterparty's perspective, and the quantum of the opportunity, Liberty transforms management's posture from anxiety to excitement. That excitement shows up in every bidder interaction. That commitment is valued.

4. **Without Liberty, you get caution. Bidders read caution as risk.** An uninformed team defaults to self-protection and guarded responses. Sophisticated bidders price that in negatively. The difference between a confident, aligned management team and a guarded, uncertain one is directly reflected in bid quality and deal outcomes.

### Why this matters for the selling board

The selling board wants the highest price and the most certain completion. Management commitment — expressed through documented financial reinvestment — directly affects what bidders are willing to pay. When bidders have that commitment in place, they compete harder and stay engaged through to final bid. When they don't, they price the doubt in. Liberty creates the condition for better bids. *(Updated 2026-04-17 — chain now lands explicitly on price, not just "bidder confidence")*

*(Source: Bill Maiden, 15 April 2026 — "management investment is a signal to the buyer that 'I believe.' That's powerful for bidders who know management will always know more than them")*

## The Life-Changing Quantum

*(Updated 2026-04-15 ~18:30 AEST, Project ES email refinement session)*

A critical and frequently underappreciated element: management teams in large buyout transactions often have **no conception of how large the MIP can be**. The incentive pool in a significant transaction can be hundreds of millions of dollars. These are life-changing numbers — but if no one has explained them, management literally cannot conceive of the opportunity, let alone get excited about it.

**What Liberty does with this:**

- Explains the quantum in concrete, personal terms — what the incentive structure means for each leader individually, not just as an abstract pool.
- Builds genuine excitement and commitment to getting a transaction done. When management understands what "the other side" looks like for them personally, it transforms how they show up.
- But it's not just economics — it's the full package: MIP terms, good leaver protections, investment structure, employment terms. Liberty seeks to craft an outcome that is enduring across the deal lifecycle.

**Tactical note for messaging:** When positioning with a Group CEO or board, do NOT quote specific MIP numbers (e.g. "$150m pool") if the recipient isn't a beneficiary. Frame as "life-changing" and "numbers management can't conceive of without explanation." The specific quantum is for management conversations, not board-level positioning.

*(Source: Bill Maiden, 15 April 2026 — "in this example, the pool could be $150 million. Often they can't conceive of how big these numbers can be in large buyout transactions")*

## CEO Bandwidth

*(Updated 2026-04-15 ~18:30 AEST, Project ES email refinement session)*

A distinct, practical argument that Group CEOs feel immediately.

The CEO's role in a sale process is all-consuming. Key responsibilities — keeping the business performing, presenting the plan to bidders — can only be done by the CEO. At the same time, the management package negotiation is likely one of the most financially significant events across the leadership team's careers, involving sophisticated counterparties where market terms are opaque and vary significantly.

Liberty takes the management terms workstream off the CEO's plate with specialist support — freeing them to focus on the things only they can do.

**Why this resonates with Group CEOs specifically:** A Group CEO knows their divisional CEO's bandwidth is finite and already stretched during a sale. Adding an unfamiliar, high-stakes negotiation on top is a practical problem, not just a fairness issue. Liberty solves it.

*(Source: Bill Maiden original email, 15 April 2026 — retained as distinct value prop through iteration)*

## Key Message 1: "Why Now" — Early Engagement with Management

**Context:** Group CEO or board resists introducing a management-side advisor early in a sale process, viewing it as premature or adding complexity.

**The framing line:**

> We're removing risk, not adding it. A better-informed, energised management team will sell the business more effectively and deliver a stronger price.

**Supporting arguments:**

- **Process moves fast and buy-in can't be retrofitted.** Once live, there's no pause button. Management needs to be prepared so that every engagement with bidders and their advisors lands well. Every interaction is being assessed.
- **Without guidance, teams default to fear.** The unfamiliarity and uncertainty of these processes cause self-protection and guarded responses. Buyers price that in — just as they do when they see conviction and confidence.
- **When the team understands what the other side looks like for them personally, they drive the process themselves.** That shift — from something being done *to* them to something they own — changes every room they walk into. It could be life-changing for them.

**Why Liberty specifically (not a lawyer, not the sell-side bank):**
- The sell-side advisor works for the seller — management knows this and calibrates trust accordingly. An independent advisor working for *them* produces authentic confidence, not rehearsed performance.
- Liberty's principals have been the management team in these processes. They don't theorise about what it feels like — they've lived the uncertainty, the fear, and the upside. That credibility lands differently with a management team than advice from a lawyer or banker.

*(Source: Bill Maiden, 15 April 2026 — developed in context of Ausgrid smart meters spin-out, applicable to any deal)*

## Key Message 2: "We Already Have Lawyers / Investment Bank Advisors"

**Context:** Management or the CEO says they are getting advice from their lawyer or the company's corporate advisor (investment bank), and questions why they need Liberty.

**The core response:**

Lawyers advise on legal terms. Investment banks advise on transaction structure and price. Neither has been the CEO sitting across the table from a PE sponsor negotiating their own management package, their own equity roll, their own employment terms. Liberty has.

**Supporting arguments:**

- **Lawyers protect you from downside; they don't optimise your upside.** A lawyer will tell you whether a clause is enforceable. They won't tell you which terms the sponsor actually cares about, which ones are negotiable theatre, and where the real value sits — because they haven't been the principal.
- **The investment bank works for the seller (or the buyer).** Their incentive is to close the transaction. Management's interests are adjacent to the deal, not central to the bank's mandate. Liberty's only client is the management team.
- **We know what the PE sponsor values in a management team — because we've been on their side too.** We know how to position the team, structure the package, and present conviction in a way that makes the sponsor want to back *this* team. We help you put together the best package the sponsor can offer — not just react to what's put in front of you.
- **Knowledge of what matters vs. what doesn't.** In any management package negotiation, there are terms that feel important but aren't, and terms that look minor but compound into significant value. Lawyers price every clause equally. Liberty knows the hierarchy because we've negotiated from the principal's chair.

**The summary line:**

> Your lawyer keeps you safe. Your bank gets the deal done. We make sure *you* come out of this in the strongest possible position — because we've been exactly where you are.

*(Source: Bill Maiden, 15 April 2026 — Liberty differentiator articulation)*

## Key Message 3: "The Other Side" — What Life Looks Like Post-Transaction

**Context:** Management team is anxious about a PE transaction because they can only see what they're losing (autonomy, certainty, the status quo), not what they're gaining.

**The core message:**

The best PE-backed management teams describe the transaction as the moment their career went from "running a business" to "building wealth." Liberty helps each leader see what that looks like for them personally — equity participation, performance incentives, career acceleration, and optionality they don't currently have.

**Supporting arguments:**

- PE sponsors *want* aligned, motivated management. The package exists to make you a partner, not an employee. Most management teams under-negotiate because they don't understand this dynamic.
- The equity roll and co-invest opportunity — even at modest levels — can generate multiples of your annual salary over a 3–5 year hold. Liberty helps you understand and maximise this.
- Career optionality expands: PE-backed CEOs and senior leaders build track records that are valued across the PE ecosystem. One good outcome opens doors to the next.

*(Source: Bill Maiden, 15 April 2026 — derived from Liberty Perennial thesis and principal experience)*

## Email Architecture — Equipping Internal Champions

*(Added 2026-04-15 ~18:30 AEST, Project ES email iteration session)*

When Liberty's contact is an internal champion (e.g. divisional CEO) who must sell upward to a decision-maker (e.g. Group CEO), the outreach email needs to be structured as a **brief FOR the champion**, not a **pitch TO them**. The champion is already on side. They need ammunition they can deliver verbally.

### Principles (derived from Project ES iteration)

1. **Open with empathy for the objection, then reframe.** If the decision-maker has said "not yet," acknowledge that it's a reasonable instinct before explaining why early engagement removes risk rather than adds it. Don't lead with a wall of counter-arguments.

2. **Lead with the actual objection, not the general value prop.** If the gating issue is timing, the timing argument goes first. The capability case is supporting material, not the headline.

3. **Compress talking points to what can be delivered in one conversation.** A champion gets one shot — a corridor conversation, a 5-minute slot in a meeting. They need 2–3 sharp, distinct arguments, not four overlapping sections that require study.

4. **Use plain language the champion can relay in their own voice.** If the email contains phrases like "multi-faceted and complex" or "engenders confidence," the champion won't paraphrase them — which means they won't use them. Write lines a CEO would actually say.

5. **The conviction chain should be the lead argument.** Management investment signals "I believe" → bidders trust insiders → better deal outcomes. This is the sharpest, most distinctive argument Liberty has. Lead with it.

6. **CEO bandwidth is a distinct, practical argument.** Group CEOs feel it immediately — their divisional CEO's time is finite, the sale is already consuming it, adding unfamiliar negotiations on top is a real problem. Keep this as a standalone point.

7. **Board duty of care is a closing line, not a standalone section.** It reinforces the decision ("this is also the responsible thing to do") but doesn't drive it. One sentence in the close.

8. **Frame the investment bank introduction as strategic validation.** The Group CEO's trusted advisor endorsing or at least understanding Liberty's role is powerful. Don't position it as a casual aside — explain why it matters ("better they have context than form a view without it").

9. **Attach the deck; don't embed the detail in the email.** The email's job is a framework and three things to say. The deck does the heavy lifting on detail.

### Anti-patterns to avoid

- Pitching the champion (they're already sold)
- Linearising parallel workstreams (some are consequential, others discrete — don't imply a sequential process)
- Quoting specific MIP numbers to non-beneficiaries
- Burying the timing rebuttal when timing is the stated objection
- Four talking points when two would stick

*(Source: Bill Maiden + iterative refinement, Project ES email session, 15 April 2026)*
